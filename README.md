## What's Actually Inside the Handbook

Let's skip the vague promises and talk content.

The book is organized around the things that actually matter when modern systems fail: medicine, food, water, and economic survival. It's not a military manual. It's not a bunker-building guide. Suzanne wrote it as a working mom who moved from LA to rural Utah and had to figure things out herself — so the level of detail is calibrated for people who don't already have a compound and a generator.

**Medicinal remedies**: This is one of the stronger sections. You get instructions for making disinfectants at home, natural painkillers (useful for toothaches and injuries), wound treatment methods, and what the book calls "frontier penicillin" — a natural infection-fighter made from kitchen staples. There's also coverage of how to handle poisoning, cold sores, inflammation, and fungal infections.

**Food preservation**: Smoking, curing, salting, dehydrating, larding, canning, and pickling — the full range. There's also a breakdown of "forever foods" that genuinely last, including a Viking food that stays good for three years and the Native American pemmican that fur trappers and arctic explorers carried for months at a time without spoiling.

**Off-grid water**: How to build a water filter from yard materials using the same activated carbon found in gas masks. Practical, replicable, and doesn't require specialty equipment.

**Economic survival**: A section on thriving through depression-era conditions, drawing from real people who survived Argentina's 2001 economic collapse — not armchair speculation.

**Finding free land in the US**: Admittedly the most niche section, but it covers multiple states (New York, Alaska, Colorado, and others), with pros and cons for geography, proximity to cities, and disaster risk.
